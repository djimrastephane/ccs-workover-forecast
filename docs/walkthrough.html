Reading time: ~18 min Assumes: zero CCS background You'll need: a browser, no CO₂ chemistry degree
01 — The problem

You just signed up to keep 100 holes in the ground safe for thirty years.

Not oil holes. CO₂ holes. Somewhere in the world, a power plant or a cement factory is capturing its carbon dioxide instead of venting it, compressing it, and sending it down a well into a saline aquifer or a depleted reservoir a kilometre or two underground. That's carbon capture and storage — CCS — and once the CO₂ is down there, it needs to stay down there for centuries.

The well is the only engineered hole through the rock that seals it in. Everything else — the caprock, the reservoir — is geology, and geology mostly takes care of itself. The well is steel, cement, elastomer seals, and valves, all built by people, all sitting in a fluid that happens to be one of the most aggressive things you can pump down a pipe.

Why CO₂ is not just “oil, but colder”

Supercritical CO₂ forms carbonic acid with any water it touches, degrades standard elastomer seals that hydrocarbon wells tolerate for decades, and can trigger hydrate plugs, halite scale, and even microbial fouling near the wellbore — failure modes an oil engineer may never have met. The industry has under thirty years of CO₂ injection history worldwide (Sleipner, offshore Norway, started in 1996) versus a century of oil and gas data. There is no CO₂-specific version of the reliability databases oil companies take for granted.

So here is the actual job: across a fleet of wells, over a multi-decade project life, which components will fail, roughly when, how badly, and what will it cost to fix them — not for one well, but as a portfolio, so that a rig can be booked two years in advance instead of scrambled for next month. Get the forecast right and you budget calmly. Get it wrong and you either burn cash on rigs you didn't need, or discover a leak your budget never planned for.

The CCS Workover Forecast is a Monte Carlo simulation built to answer exactly that question, using the best available analogue data (mostly oil & gas reliability records, corrected for CO₂-specific degradation) honestly labelled as estimates, not measurements. It will not tell you that Well INJ‑047 fails in year 14. It will tell you that, across a thousand plausible thirty‑year futures for your fleet, you should expect somewhere between 1,400 and 1,460 rig visits, with a most-likely value you can put in a budget line.

02 — The mental model

Two ideas explain almost everything you'll see on screen.

Idea one: roll the dice a thousand times, not once

A single forecast — “this packer will fail in year 19” — is a guess dressed up as a fact. Instead, the tool draws a random failure time for every component, on every well, in every simulated run, from a distribution bounded by a pessimistic case (P10 — short life, high failure rate) and an optimistic case (P90 — long life, low failure rate). Do that five hundred or a thousand times over and a shape emerges: not one future, but a spread of futures, from which three numbers get reported everywhere in this tool:

NumberWhat it meansHow to use it
P10Only 10% of simulated futures were this cheap or this quiet. An optimistic bound.Don't budget to this — it's the lucky case.
P50The median outcome. Half of simulated futures were better, half worse.Your central planning number.
P9090% of futures were this cheap or better — only the unluckiest 10% were worse.Your contingency reserve.

Idea two: components don't age at a constant rate

A brand-new well doesn't fail randomly — it fails from installation mistakes, then it settles into a long quiet stretch, then it wears out. That three-phase shape is the classic reliability “bathtub curve,” and it's the second idea running underneath every chart in this tool:

  • Infant mortality (roughly years 1–2): installation defects, a poorly-set packer, a gauge that was never quite right. Elevated risk, shrinking fast.
  • Useful life (the long middle): failures happen, but at random, low, roughly constant rates — bad luck, not systematic decline.
  • Wear-out (final ~30% of field life): corrosion, fatigue, elastomer creep — the bill for thirty years underground comes due, and it ramps up rather than hitting a cliff.

Not every component follows this exact shape, and that turns out to matter a lot. A gas hydrate plug, for instance, is most likely right at start-up (or right after any shutdown) and fades once continuous injection is established — the mirror image of the classic curve. You'll meet that distinction again in Section 4, because the tool models four separate flow-assurance failure modes precisely because one shape can't describe all of them.

Why this matters before you touch a single slider

Every input in the next section either shifts a well's position on this curve (a legacy well starting mid-life), multiplies the curve's height (a scenario, a contaminant level, a seismic event), or changes how quickly a developing problem gets caught (a monitoring tier). Once you can see the curve in your head, the sidebar stops being forty unrelated sliders and becomes one coherent story.

03 — Configuring a run

The sidebar, top to bottom, in the order it actually asks you questions.

Everything lives in one scrolling sidebar on the left of the app. It reads like a decision sequence — how big is the fleet, how old is it, how sour is the gas, how shaky is the ground, how good is your monitoring, how patient is your maintenance planner — and each answer reshapes the simulation before you ever click Run.

1

Asset configuration — how big, how long

Total well count, the injector-to-monitoring-well split, the operating life in years, and the calendar year the field starts injecting. This is the skeleton everything else hangs off: more wells means more independent dice rolls per year; a longer field life pushes more components into the wear-out phase before the simulation ends.

2

Fleet age mix — not every well is born the same year

Plenty of CCS projects don't drill new wells — they convert old oil and gas producers. A well converted at age 15 doesn't start on the bathtub curve at year zero; it starts fifteen years in, which usually means skipping infant mortality entirely and stepping straight into (or near) the wear-out ramp. Set what fraction of the fleet is “legacy” and at what age they're converted, and the tool tracks each well's own position on the curve individually — a mixed fleet genuinely behaves differently from an all-new one.

3

Scenario, stream quality, and seismicity — the three ways the ground rules can change

A scenario preset (Base Case, Conservative Design, High Corrosion, Legacy Well Conversion, and others) applies a blanket multiplier reflecting overall design philosophy and cost basis. CO₂ stream quality is more specific: it's about what's actually in the gas coming from the capture facility. Pipeline-grade CO₂ (under 10 ppm H₂S) barely moves the needle; a raw or sour stream (over 200 ppm H₂S) multiplies corrosion-sensitive components by up to 2.5× and injectivity-related failures by up to 1.8× — because hydrogen sulphide in water is genuinely nasty to carbon steel and cement. Induced seismicity is the odd one out: unlike every other failure mode, which rolls dice independently per well, a seismic event is a single roll that can strike every well in the fleet in the same year, sharply multiplying casing and cement-barrier risk when it happens.

4

Monitoring, campaigns, and equipment coverage — how you respond

The monitoring programme (Minimal / Standard / Comprehensive) sets how likely you are to catch a developing problem before it becomes an emergency — a detected failure gets rescheduled as a planned job at 80% of the reactive cost, instead of an unplanned callout. Campaign rules decide how patient the maintenance planner is: how many deferred jobs pile up before a batch rig visit is worth mobilising, and how long any single job is allowed to wait. Fleet equipment coverage lets you say that not every well has every sensor — a common reality on older or mixed-vintage assets.

Then you press the blue button

Hitting Run Simulation sets the whole machine going: sample a failure time for every component on every well in every simulated year → adjust for bathtub phase, scenario, stream quality, and any seismic event that year → roll the dice on whether it actually fails → roll again on whether monitoring catches it early → apply the barrier hierarchy to decide if it's an emergency or can wait → batch the ones that can wait into campaigns → total up the cost. All of it, a thousand times over, in under a minute.

04 — Reading the results

Ten tabs, but you really only need to read them in this order.

The results appear as a row of tabs. They're built to be read top-down the first time — start broad, then drill in only where something looks interesting.

1

Overview — the executive summary

Four KPI cards tell you almost everything a manager needs: expected rig visits (P50 and P90), the peak number of wells needing attention in the worst single year, expected batch campaigns, P50 and P90 lifecycle cost, and which component is driving the most risk. Below that, a coloured health bar per component and a plain-English narrative — the same numbers, said out loud.

2

Lifecycle Forecast — the shape of thirty years

This is Section 2's fan chart, made real. The solid line is P50 demand per year; the shaded band is the P10–P90 uncertainty range. Notice the dip after year 2 (infant mortality clearing) and the steepening climb after roughly year 21 (wear-out). Scroll down and you'll find the bathtub curve itself, plotted against the simulated failure rate — a direct check that the model is behaving the way the theory says it should.

3

Risk & Failure Modes — where to look first

A likelihood-versus-consequence matrix places every component on a five-by-five grid — the further into the dark red corner, the more it deserves attention. Below it, the lifecycle heatmap is worth lingering on: watch the Injectivity · Hydrate Control row glow red in the first few years, then cool to green, while Carbonate Scaling and Microbial Plugging stay quiet early and creep up late. Four different physical failure mechanisms, four different timing signatures, all visible in one chart — this is the payoff of not lumping every flow-assurance problem into one bucket.

4

Campaign Planning — how the work actually gets scheduled

Deferred jobs don't get executed one at a time — they queue until the campaign threshold is hit or the maximum deferral window expires, then go out as a single rig mobilisation. This tab shows the expected number of campaigns per year by type (deferred batch, urgent, emergency) and the cost each type carries — emergencies are red for a reason: no shared mobilisation, no discount, no warning.

5

Economics — where the money actually goes

A waterfall breaks total lifecycle cost into full workovers, rigless interventions, light interventions, mobilisation, and deferred-injection losses — usually full workovers dominate, because pulling a rig is expensive no matter what you find. The histogram beside it is the same thousand simulated futures as a distribution: P10, P50, and P90 marked as vertical lines through the same data you saw as three numbers on the Overview tab.

6

Well Journey — the forensic view, one well at a time

Everything above is a portfolio view. Well Journey drops you into a single simulated well's entire thirty-year service history: a health-percentage chart per component (100% at commissioning, dipping toward each failure, partially recovering after each repair — because a squeeze-cemented casing doesn't come back to 100%, it comes back to maybe 85%), followed by a plain-English story card for every single event. Click “Why did this happen?” on any card and it shows you the actual failure probability, the bathtub multiplier, and the dice roll that triggered it. This is where the model stops being a statistic and starts reading like an incident log for a well that hasn't been drilled yet.

Four more tabs round things out for specific audiences: Scenario Comparison stacks multiple runs side by side once you've tried a few configurations; Field Calibration shows how real observed field data, where you have it, pulls the generic assumptions toward reality; Assumptions is the full MTTF database with sourcing and confidence ratings for every number, for anyone who wants to audit rather than trust; and Simulation Trace is the raw event-by-event ledger behind every chart, filterable and exportable, for when “trust me” isn't good enough.

06 — What this model admits, out loud

A forecast that hides its own uncertainty is worse than no forecast.

The Model QA tab carries a calibration score of roughly 38 out of 100 — and that's deliberate honesty, not a bug. CCS has fewer than fifty commercial-scale injection wells worldwide and at most thirty years of operating history. Every MTTF value in this tool is drawn from hydrocarbon-service analogue data, adjusted by expert judgement for CO₂-specific degradation, because no CCS-only reliability database exists yet. Treat every output as an order-of-magnitude planning estimate, not an engineering commitment — and treat the Field Calibration tab as the mechanism by which real observed data, as your project accumulates it, progressively earns the right to override the generic literature assumptions.

The honest version, in one line

This tool tells you the shape and rough scale of the risk you're carrying — not the exact year a specific well will need a rig. Use it to plan budgets and rig contracts years ahead; don't use it to skip a real integrity inspection.

The words you'll see everywhere, defined once.

TermPlain-English meaning
MTTFMean Time To Failure — the average expected lifespan of a component, in years, before it fails.
Bathtub curveThe three-phase failure-rate shape over a component's life: infant mortality, useful life, wear-out.
Barrier classHow critical a component is: safety (never deferred if reactive), production, monitoring, or flow assurance.
ReactiveAn unplanned failure — nobody caught it coming.
